*A text-based, 1980s-flavored research environment*

![Back to the Future]({{"/assets/back2future.jpg" | absolute_url}})

For the past decade, my workflow has been migrating, slowly but steadily, into plain text in the terminal. What began as a Vim-and-Pandoc setup for writing has accreted, layer by layer, into something closer to a full integrated environment. The truth is that beyond Linux ricing glee, I find the use of [plain text files](https://programminghistorian.org/en/lessons/sustainable-authorship-in-plain-text-using-pandoc-and-markdown) in a keyboard-driven terminal environment is simply more conductive to thought and scholarship. And I still believe that VIM is the most powerful writing tool ever devised.  

This post is organized around the programs themselves (all developed with LLMs). The center of gravity is something that I call the Lurking deck, a small launcher I wrote to greet me on every shell. Most of the other tools below appear in its menu.

## The Lurking (Horror) deck

The first thing I see when I open a terminal is something I call **Lurking DEck**, named after the state-of-the-art computer terminal in the opening scene of *The Lurking Horror*, Infocom's 1987 MIT-set Lovecraftian text adventure. It is a small Python curses program that draws an amber-phosphor menu against a black background, with a header banner, today's date, and a numbered list of the half-dozen options: my email, the recent-files browser, the RSS news reader, the file manager, the notepad. Each is one keypress away.

![Infocom's Luring Fear. Opening scene.](/assets/infocom-lurking.png)

I think of this less as a productivity feature than as a small daily ritual and brings some joy to my daily routine. (The address, by the way, is a homeage to the computer in *Another World*, one of the most beautiful games ever created). A bullet-pointed list of the day's tasks, presented in the visual idiom of the fictional 1987 MIT terminal login, is somehow easier to take seriously than the same list in a modern reminders app or contemporary obsession with productivity. And there's nothing like a modicum of cosmic dread to get you in the mood of editing footnotes.  

Lurking reads its menu from a plain text file at `~/.config/lurking/items`, one item per line, with four pipe-delimited fields: a hotkey character, a short label, a longer description, and the shell command to run when the entry is selected. To add a new destination — a new project, a new tool, a new database I want to access from cold start — I edit the text file, add a line, save it, and the deck picks up the change next launch. There is no graphical preferences pane, no settings tab, no JSON schema to validate. Editing the menu *is* editing a text file, and I do it in the same editor I do everything else. (More on the editor in a moment.)

![The Lurking Deck](/assets/lurking-deck.png)

The deck runs on every interactive shell launch through one guarded line in my `.zshrc` — a one-line addition that took me longer to think of than to type — and it is also bound to `Mod+Alt+L` in my Sway configuration, so I can summon it from any other workspace as well. The result is that the deck is never more than one keystroke away. It is the closest thing I have to a desktop, in a system that otherwise has no desktop. This is, I think, how a desktop ought to feel: a list of doors, in plain text, each labeled with where it goes.

A non-obvious detail: most of Lurking's menu entries are not just commands but commands wrapped in an Alacritty invocation that itself wraps a login shell. The pattern is `alacritty -e bash -l -c "…"`. The reason is that the destination programs — Vim, Ranger, the email clients — depend on environment variables, shell aliases, or shell function definitions that exist only inside a fully-loaded login shell. Without the wrapper, half the entries would silently fail to find their dependencies. The wrapper itself is a kind of small philosophical commitment: every door on the deck opens onto a fresh terminal with a fully loaded environment, indistinguishable from one I would have set up by hand.

## File browsing: rf and Ranger

The single most-used entry on the Lurking deck is **`rf`** — short for "recent files." It is the rare custom tool that has actually changed how I work. It is a bash function, perhaps thirty lines including its preview logic, that scans my main working directories for anything I have touched in the last few days and presents the list inside `fzf`, the fuzzy finder, with a live preview pane that adapts to whatever file is selected.

Highlight a PDF: the first page of text appears in the preview, extracted on the fly. This is enormously useful for triaging recently-downloaded articles — you can read the title, the abstract, and the affiliation block without ever opening a PDF viewer. Highlight a Markdown file: the document renders with light syntax coloring. Highlight a `.docx`: Pandoc converts it to plain text on the fly so I can remember what is inside without launching LibreOffice.

And here is the part I love. Highlight a JPEG: the image appears in the preview pane as colored ASCII. The conversion is done by a tool called `jp2a`, which samples the image and emits character-cell art, in color, with whatever palette the terminal supports. A photograph reduced to text characters looks a little like a woodcut of itself, and the abstraction is half the charm. There are sophisticated image-preview protocols out there — kitty's, sixel, Überzug — but they all carry tradeoffs, and I am on Wayland, where most of them simply do not work. The text-as-image solution sidesteps the whole question.

You can read this as iconoclasm if you want — and I half-mean it that way, see below — but it is also just *correct*. The preview is faster than a real image preview. It works everywhere. It composes through pipes the way every other terminal tool composes. And there is a kind of aesthetic uniformity that comes from rendering everything in the same medium: PDFs, prose, photographs, all in characters.

When `rf` is not enough — when I need to actually move into a directory, batch-rename files, or copy things between locations — I switch to **Ranger**, the venerable terminal file manager. Ranger's keybindings are essentially Vim's, which is the whole point. After enough years of `j` and `k` for everything, browsing files any other way feels obscurely wrong. Ranger has a three-pane "Miller column" layout: parent directory on the left, current directory in the middle, preview of the highlighted file on the right. The preview pane is configured to use the same `jp2a` trick as `rf`, so every image in a directory is previewed as ASCII as I scroll past it. The aesthetic match between `rf` and Ranger is not accidental. It is the same idea, applied at two different scopes — the recent-files inbox and the full filesystem.

Both tools dispatch their selections through `xdg-open`, which routes by MIME type. PDFs go to Zathura. Markdown goes to Vim. Images go to my configured viewer. The same pattern, the same dispatch, the same plumbing — across two programs that look and feel like one extended interface.

## DAVID's IRE: my Vim configuration

The center of gravity of all this — at least, of all the *writing* — is Vim. I have been using it for years, and the configuration file at this point is part diary, part inventory, part haunted house: bindings I no longer remember adding, plugins I no longer remember installing, comments addressed to a future self who turns out to have been me last Thursday. The file opens with an ASCII banner that names it, somewhat self-importantly, **DAVID's IRE** — for Integrated Research Environment, but also an honest acknowledgment of what Vim periodically produces in its user.

What I actually use Vim for is writing prose. Markdown source, plain text with light syntactic markup, compiled to PDF through Pandoc when I need a polished output. This pairing is the foundation, and it has held up through every fashion in writing software for the last decade. Markdown is portable — every tool reads it, every future tool will read it, and a file from 2015 is exactly as legible today as the day it was written. Pandoc does the rest: citations resolved against a BibTeX library, footnotes formatted, a bibliography appended at the end. The result is indistinguishable from anything produced in Word with a reference manager. The source, however, is something I can edit on a phone if I have to, and will still be able to edit in twenty years.

A few things make the Vim side of this pleasant.

The Pandoc plugin gives me citation autocomplete: I type `@auth` and a tab-completion menu offers all the matching keys from my BibTeX library. A single master `.bib` file, several thousand entries, and the editor itself becomes the citation-insertion interface that reference managers like Zotero or EndNote spend whole UI panes trying to provide. It costs nothing and disappears into the act of writing.

A pair of custom commands, bound to `F11` and `F12`, compile the current Markdown buffer to PDF using a Chicago note-bibliography CSL and either spawn a new Zathura window or refresh an existing one. The clever bit, which I would not have come up with on my own, is the use of a `pkill -HUP zathura` signal: Zathura interprets a hangup as "reload the current document," so the same key sequence does the right thing whether or not a preview is already open. Press the key, glance at the next monitor, keep typing.

A small function I cobbled together looks at whatever BibTeX key the cursor is sitting on and finds the corresponding PDF anywhere in my reference library. Cursor on `@vermeule2017`, two keystrokes (`,op`), and the article opens in Zathura. With a library of several thousand PDFs, this is the difference between "let me check that page reference" being a thirty-second context switch and being a two-second one. It is the function I would miss most if it were taken away.

When Vim opens with no argument, a plugin called Startify takes over the buffer. I use it for two things. The first is bookmarks: four pinned files I touch every day — my master writing index, my courses scratchpad, my daily log, and the Vim configuration itself — each opened with a single letter (`a`, `b`, `c`, `d`). The second is silly and important. A small shell pipeline picks a random line from a text file of aphorisms by Nicolás Gómez Dávila — the dark antimodernist Colombian philosopher whose *escolios* are some of the most condensed prose in Spanish — and pipes it through `cowsay`, the venerable Unix toy that draws a cow saying whatever you put in front of it. Each Vim launch greets me with a different reactionary micro-meditation, framed by a cartoon cow. It is exactly as ridiculous as it sounds, and I would not part with it.

![Startify screen showing a Dávila aphorism inside a cowsay frame, above a list of bookmarked files](images/startify-davila.png)
*A representative morning. Different aphorism each launch, same cow.*

Two further pieces of the IRE are worth dwelling on, because they are about making the editing buffer pleasant to look at — and pleasant to *think* in, which is the same thing one degree removed.

The first is the rendering of nested list bullets as different Unicode glyphs at three indentation levels: `◉` for top-level items, `○` for second-level, `◆` for third. The underlying source remains plain `- item`, perfectly portable to any other tool, but on screen the bullets stratify into a small visual hierarchy. The intent is to approximate, in plain vanilla Vim, the kind of beautified rendering that [render-markdown.nvim](https://github.com/MeanderingProgrammer/render-markdown.nvim) provides for Neovim users. That plugin is gorgeous, and I would happily use it; my home-grown approximation gets perhaps half its visual effect through regular expressions and Vim's built-in `matchadd` mechanism, and half is enough. Outlines that look like outlines are outlines you actually finish.

The second is a small set of syntax-highlighting overrides geared specifically toward academic prose in Pandoc. Two things get marked off from the surrounding text in scholarly writing: italicized words (titles, foreign terms, emphasis) and double-quoted phrases (terms of art, scare-quotes, glossed citations). I have configured each to display in its own distinct color — italics inherit the `Constant` highlight group, the color a code editor would use for string or numeric literals, while quoted phrases inherit `Question`, a contrasting accent typically used for prompts. The vim-pandoc plugin handles this badly out of the box: it tries to conceal quotation marks with Unicode replacements that fight with the rest of the editing experience. My configuration first clears vim-pandoc's default `inlineQuote` syntax definition and replaces it with a custom regex that matches `"…"` sequences without the misbehavior, then redirects the highlight group. The result is that scanning a chapter visually, I can see at a glance where my emphases and my quotations are. For a writing form that depends so heavily on both, this is more useful than it sounds.

This is the manifesto's first plank applied at the level of the buffer: aesthetics as a cognitive tool. Pretty rendering is not decoration. Note-taking and outlining become a pleasure to behold, and prose that you take pleasure in looking at is prose you return to.

The rest of DAVID's IRE is small comforts accumulated over years. Curly-brace placeholders like `{TODO-FIND-CITATION}` highlight as errors and get collected into a quickfix list with a single keystroke, making the pre-submission sweep mechanical. A leader-key mapping (`,e` and `,ñ`) toggles between English and Spanish spell-checking. Another (`,ss`) inserts a long s `ſ` for the early modern texts I quote. `F5` inserts the date in the format my daily log expects. `jj` exits insert mode without my having to reach for `Esc`. None of these is exotic. All of them are Vim doing what Vim does. The point is that none of them require a plugin ecosystem to maintain.

## Notepad and the OM/AW philosophy

The fifth entry on the Lurking deck is labeled `NOTEPAD`, and it points at a small bash script I wrote called `nv.sh`. The script implements a capture workflow that sits on top of a deliberately old-fashioned philosophy of note-taking that I have come to call **OM/AW** — for *Org-Mode / Always Writing* — and which has become, over the last year or so, the structuring principle of how I work.

Let me start with the script and then circle back to the philosophy.

The flow is this. Pressing `5` on the deck — or `Mod+Alt+N` from anywhere in Sway — launches the script, which opens a fresh Vim session in a floating Alacritty window against a brand-new empty buffer. Vim drops me directly into insert mode with a custom amber-brown colorscheme called `brown-notes`, derived from the Brown colorscheme already in my plugin list, and with the filetype set to `markdown.pandoc`. That filetype activates the full IRE feature set inside the scratchpad: BibTeX completion, conceal-rendered emphasis, the ◉/○/◆ bullet glyphs, the academic-prose syntax highlighting, all of it. I type whatever the thought is. I save and quit. The script then appends the buffer's contents to a single master file at `~/Documents/nvdata/notes2025.md`, separated from the previous entry by a blank line. The temp file is deleted. The window closes itself. The whole interaction takes seconds. No file naming. No folder choosing. No metadata to fill in. Just: thought, capture, append.

The destination — that single master file — is where the philosophy comes in.

**OM/AW** prescribes that every writing project, whether an article, a chapter, or a book, lives in a single plain-text file. Notes, outlines, presentations, drafts, and the eventual final piece all live in that one file. The Org-Mode half of the name is a debt to Carsten Dominik's Emacs system, which was the first widely-used environment to demonstrate that a single deeply-structured text file can serve as inbox, planner, outline, and finished document at once. The "Always Writing" half is the operational principle: every keystroke I commit to the file is, in principle, headed somewhere. There is no separate phase for note-taking, no separate phase for outlining, no separate phase for drafting. Notes become outline points. Outline points become paragraphs. Paragraphs become the published thing. The flow is continuous, and the file is its substrate.

In practice, then, my working directory looks like this. There is a single rolling notes file — `notes2025.md`, captured into by the Notepad script — for thoughts that have not yet found a project. And there is one `project-name.md` per active piece of writing: one per article, one per chapter, one per book. The article-level file is the unit of structure, because the article is the unit of finishing. (The structuring principle of the whole system, ultimately, is *publishing*. Every file is a place I expect to ship something from.)

Each project file follows a loose convention. A handful of section headings recur across most of them:

- **OUTLINE** — the bulk of the research, structured as a deeply nested list, three or four levels deep. Most thinking happens here.
- **ABSTRACTS** — since a single file often contains several research outcomes (a journal article, a conference paper, a talk drawn from the same material), there are often several abstracts in different registers and lengths.
- **INPUT** — a stream-of-consciousness section where I jot down anything new before deciding where it belongs.
- **Draft1**, **Draft2**, **Draft3** — successive revisions, kept rather than overwritten.
- **CopyEdits** — corrected versions after editorial review.
- **TALKS** — bullet-point versions of the material adapted for oral delivery.

What makes this tractable in a single file, rather than across the dozen files some other workflow would distribute it across, is Vim's navigation. The vim-pandoc plugin's folding turns a 5,000-line file into a single screen of section headings, each fold a single line. The plugin's `:TOC` command (mapped to `Ctrl-T` in the IRE) opens a navigable table of contents in a side window — click or `Enter` on any heading to jump there. Together these are, I think, the killer features that make OM/AW feasible. Without them the file would be unmanageable; with them it is a delight.

A small recent realization, which I am still working out the implications of, is about tagging. Most note-taking systems require some kind of tag syntax: `#concept` in Markdown, `:tag:` in Org-Mode, `[[wikilink]]` in Roam-style tools. The point of these tags is to make concepts navigable across the corpus. But Vim already has a more fundamental version of the same operation: the `*` command, which searches for the word under the cursor across the current file, and `:vimgrep` (or `Ag`, my preferred ripgrep frontend), which extends the search to a directory.

The realization is that if I write proper nouns and key concepts in **CamelCase** — *NativeExegesis*, *SacredAntiquarianism*, *CodexVaticanusA* — then `*` and `:vimgrep` are doing exactly the work that an explicit tag system would, without any tag syntax at all. The CamelCase token *is* the tag. It is searchable, navigable, and unambiguous (no other word looks like *NativeExegesis*). It composes naturally with prose: a sentence can mention the concept while in the act of being a sentence. And — pleasingly — Tim Pope's `vim-abolish` plugin can convert CamelCase to `snake_case`, `kebab-case`, or `Title Case` with a single command, should I ever want to refactor my notation. The whole hypertextual edifice that systems like Roam and Logseq erect — backlinks panels, graph views, tag indexes — collapses into Vim's existing primitives. I have not yet stopped being delighted by this.

The broader argument I would make for OM/AW is historical. The fragmented note-taking systems that dominate today — atomic notes in Obsidian, the Zettelkasten method, the Unix discipline of one-file-per-thing — were optimal solutions to constraints that no longer apply. The Unix directory hierarchy was a way to organize information when each file consumed precious inodes and `ls` was your only retrieval interface. Niklas Luhmann's Zettelkasten was a way to organize a hundred thousand notes when those notes were paper index cards in a wooden cabinet, retrievable only by hand-traversing a chain of cross-references. Even the early Org-Mode workflow assumed a machine where loading a 50-megabyte text file might be too slow.

None of these constraints applies any longer. RAM is plentiful. Text is cheap. Vim's navigation, fuzzy finding through `fzf`, ripgrep search, and full-text search through plugins like `:Ack` make a 50,000-line file as navigable as a directory of 500 small ones — possibly more so, since the relationships between the parts are spatial rather than nominal. And the rise of LLM-based knowledge tools (NotebookLM, custom GPTs trained on a corpus, RAG pipelines) makes a single rich text file an asset rather than a liability: feed the whole thing to a model and ask questions of it. The fragmented-corpus systems struggle with this kind of holistic interrogation because they were designed for human retrieval rather than machine ingestion. A single large text file is the easiest thing in the world to ingest.

So the move toward OM/AW is partly aesthetic, partly practical, and partly an acknowledgment that we have been organizing knowledge as if hardware constraints from 1985 were still binding. They are not.

To make a project file presentable when I do need to print or share it — for a workshop talk, a draft circulation, a final submission — every project file gets a YAML preamble that configures Pandoc's LaTeX output. The preamble sets the bibliography path, the citation style, sensible page geometry, the Gentium Plus font (one of the few open-source fonts with reliable coverage of Spanish, Nahuatl orthography, and IPA), and a small handful of LaTeX customizations: framed pull-quotes, kw-style boxed inline code, non-default bullet glyphs that match the on-screen IRE rendering, and hanging footnotes. The result is that a single keystroke (`F11`) takes a working OM/AW file and produces a PDF that looks like something I would put on an academic CV.

The colorscheme rotates. Currently I am on Gruvbox light with hard contrast, but the configuration file keeps Seoul256, Nord, Synthwave84, Neon, Brown, Synthwave, and Tokyonight loaded and ready. Different times of day call for different palettes; different drafts call for different moods.

## Newsboat and rdrview

I follow somewhere over a hundred RSS feeds — blogs, journals, news sites, a handful of personal sites whose authors I want to keep tabs on. **Newsboat**, my reader of choice, makes this volume feel like reading rather than work.

Newsboat is, by default, perfectly competent. The configuration I have layered on top of it is mostly cosmetic: Vim-style keybindings (`h`, `j`, `k`, `l` for navigation, `J` and `K` for jumping between feeds, `G` and `g` for end and home), a color scheme that separates unread from read at a glance, syntax highlighting for URLs and titles inside articles. The single best decision in the configuration, however, is the way Newsboat opens links. I have it pipe every clicked URL through a tool called **`rdrview`**, a Readability-style content extractor that strips a webpage down to its main content — the way Firefox Reader View does — and then renders the result through Lynx, the long-standing text browser. Press one key on any RSS item and you get the actual article: no sidebars, no cookie banners, no autoplay video, no chrome. Most blog posts and news articles read better this way than they do on the actual website. A few read better than they did when their authors first published them.

This is also a small reproach to where the web has ended up. Reader View exists because the modern article-as-marketing-funnel is unreadable in its native form. We have collectively built a medium so cluttered that every browser ships a button to undo the clutter. The honest move is to skip the cluttered version and go straight to the extracted one — and if you can do it inside a terminal, all the better.

## Email: a brief homage and a current arrangement

Of all the pieces of this setup, email is the one I have changed my mind about most often. The full odyssey would take a separate post; here is the short version.

I started, years ago, with **Alpine** — the descendant of Pine, the venerable terminal mail client that taught a generation how to read email through SSH. Alpine is one of the loveliest text-mode applications ever written. Sensible defaults, gentle keybindings, beautifully terse status lines, a composition workflow that felt — even decades after the fact — exactly right. I loved it. I would have happily used Alpine for the rest of my career. Then Google made it impossible. The OAuth gauntlet for Gmail authentication grew increasingly hostile to clients that had not registered as official applications, and Alpine — maintained largely as a labor of love — could not keep up. Around 2020 I gave up and started looking elsewhere.

I tried, in roughly this order, **Mutt** (powerful but labyrinthine to configure), **`cmdg`** (excellent for Gmail specifically, but Gmail-only), and **`aerc`** (modern, full-featured, but more configuration than I want my mail client to demand). Each of them taught me something. None of them was the answer.

The answer turned out to be **Matcha**. Matcha is a newer client built on Bubble Tea, the Go TUI framework, and it represents something I had begun to think did not exist anymore: a terminal mail client you can be productive in on the second day rather than the second month. Sensible defaults. A clean three-pane layout. Composition opens in Vim. It just works. I have settled there for personal mail, and use `cmdg` only as a specialized fallback for institutional Gmail where it is the only thing that authenticates reliably.

If a Google product manager is reading this: please make life easier for the people maintaining Matcha and `cmdg`. The world needs more terminal mail, not less.

## A note on how all this got built

I should be honest about something. A non-trivial fraction of the configuration that holds this setup together — Vim functions, shell pipelines, the Lurking launcher itself — was assembled iteratively in conversation with large language models. I do not consider myself a programmer. I have not memorized vimscript syntax, and I have a working but partial grasp of bash. The fact that I can describe what I want in plain English, get a working snippet, test it, refine it, and end up with a personal environment shaped to my work is, for me, the most important software development of the last decade.

It used to be that customizing a tool at this depth required either a long apprenticeship or a willingness to copy other people's dotfiles wholesale. That barrier has effectively collapsed. The configuration is mine in the sense that the choices and the use cases are mine, and I have read and edited every line — but I would not have written most of it from scratch, and I would not have attempted half of it. The conjunction of a 1980s interaction model and a 2020s AI assistant is, somewhat unexpectedly, a remarkably productive combination.

## A short manifesto, since the post got this far

There is a temptation, when describing a setup like this one, to disclaim it. To say something like *of course this is not for everyone* or *of course modern tools have their place*. I would rather not. The terminal — text-based, keyboard-driven, Ncurses-rendered, composable through pipes — is, on every axis I care about, superior to every paradigm that came after it. The argument has a few parts.

**Aesthetics is tantamount.** The visual idiom of a working environment shapes the kind of attention you bring to it. I write differently in front of monospace amber on black than I do in front of a Word document, and the difference is not trivial. A 1980s phosphor terminal asks you to slow down, to think before typing, to commit to a sentence before you can backspace it. That is exactly the right disposition for prose. The slow, deliberate pleasures of a CRT-flavored research environment are partly nostalgic, sure, but the underlying interaction model was already mostly right by 1985. Most of what has happened since has been an accretion of distractions.

**One project, one file. Always writing.** The OM/AW philosophy described above is the structural counterpart to the aesthetic one. The fragmentation that pervades modern note-taking — the atomic-note tools, the Zettelkasten revivals, the unix discipline of one-file-per-thing — is the residue of constraints that no longer apply. Small RAM. Slow file loading. Paper index cards. None of it is binding anymore. Vim's navigation, fuzzy finding, ripgrep search, and now LLM ingestion (NotebookLM, RAG over a personal corpus, custom GPTs) make a single richly-structured text file an asset rather than a liability. The directory tree was a consequence of the inode, not a property of thought. Concentrate the work, not scatter it. Every file should be a place you expect to ship something from.

**Ncurses should be mandatory.** Anything that draws to a terminal cell grid in a way the user can predict and manipulate is a candidate for inclusion in this setup; anything that requires GPU compositing, Electron, or a 200-megabyte runtime is, by definition, not. I will admit one cheerful exception: Bubble Tea, the Go TUI framework that powers Matcha and a growing number of newer clients, is wonderful too. Anything that lets developers ship Ncurses-quality interfaces without writing C is fine by me.

**The mouse, and the use of images, are banned by Deuteronomy.** This is partly a joke and partly not. There is an iconoclastic streak running through the whole setup — a refusal of images in favor of text, of GUIs in favor of keystrokes — and the rendering of JPEGs as colored ASCII is its sincerest expression. The mouse is the graven image of human-computer interaction. It produces nothing the keyboard cannot, slower and with more wrist strain. After enough years inside Vim, you stop noticing the mouse exists.

**Email providers should be more welcoming to command-line clients.** Especially Google. The fact that `cmdg` works, that Matcha works, that `aerc` works at all in 2026 is despite the platforms, not because of them. Mutt remains the canonical TUI mail client, but its configuration is a labyrinth in the technical sense: you can get lost in it. Matcha is the antidote — useful on day two rather than month two — and I would happily campaign for the existence of more clients in its mold.

**The death of Infocom in 1989, and of the parser-driven text adventure more broadly, was a world tragedy comparable in magnitude only to the dissolution of the Soviet Union later that same year.** Both events were the foreclosure of certain imaginative possibilities. *The Lurking Horror* is one of the strangest, finest objects ever produced by a commercial software company: an MIT-set Lovecraftian text adventure that takes itself entirely seriously, written in parser-driven prose more careful than most novels of its decade. That an entire genre died because it could not compete with the *visual* thrill of *Doom* is a fact I have not entirely forgiven the 1990s for. The setup described above is, among other things, a small tribute to that lost world. Lovecraft, who knew that the unspeakable is more powerful when written than when shown, would have approved.

## Roadmap

A few pieces I have not yet pulled into the terminal but intend to.

**News, exclusively through RSS.** Newsboat already covers the bulk of my reading, but a few sites remain stubbornly outside the feed. The aim is to absorb everything. Where official feeds do not exist, scrapers can synthesize them. The endpoint is to never visit a news website manually again. The web is, when you stop visiting it, surprisingly easy to live without.

**Music in the terminal.** `ncspot` is a Rust-based TUI client for Spotify, using the official Connect API. The interface is essentially Vim-like; playback is native; the resource footprint is a fraction of the official Spotify desktop client, which, like every other Electron app, is several hundred megabytes of Chromium pretending to be a music player. I see no reason not to switch.

**WhatsApp in the terminal.** This one is harder, because Meta does not officially support third-party clients. The pragmatic options are `nchat` and `matterbridge`. Either approach is more fragile than I would like, but the alternative — keeping a Chrome tab open all day for WhatsApp Web — falls foul of the Deuteronomy clause above.

The general principle: every application that lives outside the terminal is a candidate for being moved into it. The destination is a single Sway workspace, no browser permanently open, no graphical client whose absence would be felt. We are not there yet. We are getting closer.
